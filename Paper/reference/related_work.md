Related Work
Multi-subject personalization: solving who appears in the scene
Under DiT/FLUX backbones, early multi-subject research primarily addresses a subject-centric question: who should appear in the generated scene, and how can multiple referenced identities remain distinct under shared generation dynamics. XVerse is a representative method in this direction. Its key idea is to convert reference images into token-specific text-stream modulation offsets, injecting subject-specific control along the text-conditioning pathway rather than directly perturbing image latents or visual features. This non-invasive design is particularly important in multi-subject generation, as it improves identity and attribute controllability while preserving editability and avoiding direct corruption of the shared image representation. MOSAIC tackles the same problem from a representation-centric perspective. By enforcing semantic correspondence and feature disentanglement across references, it seeks to separate subjects in feature space and reduce identity blending as the number of subjects increases. Taken together, these methods move DiT/FLUX personalization beyond simple fidelity preservation toward explicit subject separation, making “who is who” the first layer of controllable multi-subject generation.
Layout-to-region binding: solving where each subject should go
Once subject identity is preserved, the next question becomes where each subject should appear. This motivates a second line of work on layout-to-region binding, which aims to align references, prompts, and spatial regions under DiT/FLUX backbones. ContextGen is a representative training-based method in this category. It introduces Contextual Layout Anchoring (CLA) to stabilize the correspondence between instances and target positions, and Identity Consistency Attention (ICA) to preserve subject identity when multiple references must be grounded to different regions in the same image. AnyMS addresses the same challenge in a training-free manner, decoupling attention at global and local levels to jointly maintain prompt alignment, identity preservation, and layout fidelity. 3DIS-FLUX also belongs to this line, but approaches the problem through depth-guided scene construction followed by FLUX-based rendering. Its advantage is that depth offers a compact structural prior for composing multiple instances before detail synthesis. At the same time, its limitation is equally revealing: when the depth map itself underspecifies hidden geometry or front–back ordering, occlusion relations may still collapse. In this sense, layout binding methods solve “where,” but only partially address the physically harder question of what should remain visible under overlap.
Attention leakage and entanglement in DiT/FLUX: explaining why confusion happens
For a study centered on occlusion and entanglement, it is useful to interpret multi-subject failure through the lens of attention leakage. In DiT-style generators, global attention is both a strength and a liability: while it enables rich long-range interaction, it also allows identity cues, attributes, colors, and poses from one subject to leak into another when all tokens compete in a shared attention space. This issue becomes especially relevant in FLUX, whose architecture combines double-stream blocks and single-stream blocks. The double-stream stage retains partially separate pathways for text and image tokens, preserving some degree of structural separation, whereas the later single-stream stage flattens both modalities into a tightly fused sequence. As a result, the model can lose the structural inductive bias for distinct instances, making subject confusion more likely once fine-grained appearance and interaction cues are jointly modeled. From this perspective, semantic entanglement is not merely an identity-preservation failure, but an attention-routing failure. Existing methods can be understood as different attempts to mitigate this problem: XVerse achieves non-invasive subject control through text-stream modulation; MOSAIC reduces leakage through semantic alignment and disentanglement; AnyMS decouples global and local attention flows; 3DIS-FLUX and related layout-guided methods constrain attention spatially; and LayerBind further introduces layered control to preserve ownership of regions and visible order. This progression suggests that multi-subject generation in DiT/FLUX is fundamentally constrained by how well the model can route attention between subjects, regions, and modalities without cross-instance contamination.
Interaction and occlusion: solving who is in front, who is behind, and how they interact
The final and most challenging layer is physical interaction and occlusion reasoning. At this stage, the problem is no longer just who appears and where, but who occludes whom, whether partially hidden instances remain geometrically plausible, and whether the model respects front–back ordering under overlap. SeeThrough3D makes this issue explicit by arguing that prior layout-conditioned generation methods under-address the problem of ordering. Its contribution is therefore not merely stronger 3D layout control, but a mechanism for modeling which object should appear in front, which should remain behind, and how partial visibility should be rendered consistently. This connects naturally to the notion of amodal completion: even when one subject is occluded, the model should still infer the plausible structure of its hidden parts rather than dropping or distorting them. LayerBind approaches the same problem from a training-free DiT perspective. Rather than learning a specialized controller, it leverages the coarse-to-fine denoising dynamics of DiTs: layout and visible order are established at early denoising steps through layered initialization, and semantic details are refined later while respecting the chosen layering. This temporal progression makes LayerBind especially relevant for overlap-heavy scenes. Nevertheless, even these recent methods mostly reason at the level of boxes, regions, or layers. Deeply entangled interactions—such as hugging, grappling, hand contact, or interleaved articulated bodies—remain difficult, because the challenge there is not only visibility ordering, but also fine-grained contact geometry, mutual deformation, and physically consistent completion under occlusion.
Summary
Overall, DiT/FLUX research on multi-subject generation can be read as a progression from identity control (“who”), to layout binding (“where”), to attention leakage mitigation (“why subjects get entangled”), and finally to interaction and occlusion reasoning (“who is in front, who is behind, and how overlap should be rendered”). Existing methods have made substantial progress on each layer, yet a clear gap remains between semantic disentanglement and physical disentanglement. In particular, many methods can separate identities or bind subjects to regions, but still fail when subjects become spatially intertwined and their visible order, hidden geometry, and fine-grained interaction must be resolved jointly. This gap highlights the need for models that treat multi-subject generation not merely as a layout task, but as a problem of physically grounded interaction reasoning.
